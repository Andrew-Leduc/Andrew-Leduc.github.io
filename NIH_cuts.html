Basic Scientific Research: How It Works and Why It Matters

I decided to write this in light of recent government policies that threaten to devastate the world’s premier ecosystem for scientific research. These include the reduction in overhead costs to 15% on NIH grants, the firing of thousands of employees from the NIH and NHS, and the anticipated 50% reduction in the NSF budget. Many people don’t know how fundamental science is conducted, funded, or why it matters—if that sounds like you, please read on. (I discuss the specifics of overhead costs, why they matter, and why 15% is dangerously low at the end.)

What Is Basic Science?

Science is a method for investigating how the natural world works. It involves making measurements, comparing them to our current understanding, refining that understanding, and then repeating this cycle. We generally call research basic science if it does not have an immediate, obvious path to economic return—so it doesn’t attract private investment in the short term (roughly 5–10 years). Instead, governments primarily fund basic science using taxpayer dollars (plus a small percentage from charities, often incentivized by tax breaks).

Why Public Funding Matters

You might think: “If it’s not expected to have a direct real-world impact soon, why should I pay for it?” or “Why does the NIH need a \$46 billion budget? Why fund quantum gravity if the deficit is over \$36 trillion?” These are legitimate concerns. Yet, funding for basic science through the NIH and NSF is only about 1.1% of yearly tax revenue (or 0.77% of government spending in 2024).

This investment is crucial because basic science is the economic engine powering many breakthroughs. The internet, ChatGPT, antibiotics, and cancer treatments—all of these exist thanks to basic research, much of it done at universities. Consider mRNA vaccine technology: it arose from 20 years of lab studies starting in the 1990s. Likewise, modern AI relies on decades of work on neural networks and non-convex optimization— published by university researchers, not by the CEOs currently commercializing it.

Our university research system is one of the United States’ greatest strengths. It attracts talented scientists from around the world, many of whom stay here (when allowed by immigration policy). By training new scientists and spinning out cutting-edge ideas, our universities help create the most innovative industries in the world.

Concern About “Waste” and Overhead Costs

Perhaps you worry about potential waste of taxpayer dollars. That brings us to overhead costs, which have been slashed from about 35% on NIH grants to 15% under new policies. Typically, if a professor is awarded \$1 million from the NIH, the university receives an additional ~35% in “indirect costs” to pay for essential infrastructure: buildings, electricity, lab equipment, administrative staff, etc. While “indirect costs” can seem opaque, they’re critical for enabling research— especially in high-cost urban areas like Boston, New York, or San Francisco.

Cutting overhead to 15% drastically reduces a university’s ability to maintain these facilities. This move will disproportionately harm top institutions that are integral to breakthroughs in cancer research, drug discovery, and more. Even if such cuts last only a few years, they could delay vital treatments or discoveries—potentially impacting the health of countless people.

The Bigger Picture

Yes, the country faces serious financial constraints, and it’s worth reviewing how we allocate our resources. But there’s a difference between responsible oversight of research funding and an overnight slash to indirect costs after little deliberation. These changes and other cuts are already forcing hiring freezes, shutting down labs, and halting progress.

Even if you support the current administration, please recognize the damage of hasty, extreme cuts to basic science. This research ecosystem underpins not just our higher education system, but also the industries and treatments that keep us ahead in global innovation. Four years of stalled progress could mean someone doesn’t get the therapy they need in time.

Thank you for reading. If you have questions or want to learn more, consider talking to scientists in your community, looking up how NIH or NSF grants are allocated, and contacting your representatives. Ultimately, basic science is an investment in future breakthroughs that can profoundly benefit our society and economy.
