Nano proteomic sample preperation for single cell proteomics

Protocol

nPOP is a droplet based method for single cell proteomic sample preparation. We see nPOP as a conceptual framework more so than a specific workflow. In this post I will talk about why we designed nPOP, how we recommend its use, how to adapt nPOP for different workflows, and address aspects that have not been fully optimized and hopefully may be further explored by others. nPOP was developed with several goals in mind.

  • 1) Miniaturize sample preparation volumes to several nanoliters. This has a number of benefits. It enables cleaner samples by reducing the amount of reagents needed to prepare a single cell. It also enables higher sample preparation throughput by allowing users to prepare a larger number of samples with less reagents. Lastly, it can reduce sample preparation losses by exposing samples to less surface area.
  • 2) Fully automate the sample preparation. This can remove batch effects related to user error, increasing data consistency while also allowing for increased throughput by removing manual pipetting steps.
  • 3) Create a flexible workflow that allows users to maximize the number of samples simultaneously prepared and can accommodate many different experimental designs.

To achieve these goals, we took a unique approach to sample preparation, abandoning familiar staples of mass spectrometry sample preparation such as tube and wells. In our approach, single cells are prepared in nanoliter droplets on the surface of an unpatterned glass slide with a fluorocarbon coating. The is approach allows the user to create any droplet geometry desired, so the sample preparation can meet the needs of different workflows and adapt to the changing landscape of multiplexing reagents. In these droplets, we lyse cells, digest proteins to peptides, label peptides for multiplexing, and pool samples into a set. The samples are dispensed into a 384 well plate where they can be picked up by most standard autosamplers. We use the Ultimate 3000 from Thermo. To perform the sample preparation, we use the CellenONE liquid handling and cell dispensing platform made by Scienion. The system uses acoustic technology to reliably dispens cells and various reagents in droplets of ~300 picoliters.

We have designed the following workflows for multiplexing with TMT, mTRAQ, and label free. We are strong advocates of using multiplexed workflows over label free methods. The reasons for this are twofold; 1) multiplexing samples provides the opportunity to significantly increase throughput which is essential for many single cell applications and 2) labeled methods allow for transfering identifications for peptides that are plenty abundant for reliable quantitation, but insufficiently abundant for confident identification. TMT base workflows allow the user to prepare over 2000 single cells in one day (144 sets). For label free and mTRAQ based workflows, we provide templates that allow the user to prepare a similar number of single cells per day. However, a small limitation for these workflows is the sample pickup speed. As we have currently designed it, this workflow allows for the user to pickup ~ 384 samples in about 6-7 hours. The sample pickup process is very robust and hands off but certainly time consuming. If this throughput is insufficient, we have ideas about how to further increase pickup speeds. This involves increasing the speed of the robot arm's motion, decreasing the length of pooling steps, and parallelizing the pickup with both nozzles. Theoretically you should be able to pick up ~1500 LF samples in ~6 hours. If you make any progress towards this end, let me know!

There are also several other areas where we think the process can be further optimized. While the fluorocarbon surface has worked well for peptide recovery, it's possible to think of other surfaces that might also work well such as micropatterned glass. Etching patterns in borosilicate glass can give hydrophobic properties that allow nice droplet formation and further reduce contact area. While we have rigorously benchmarked the efficacy of cell lysis in 100% DMSO, certain hard to lyse cells or challenging proteins may benefit from adding additional reagents such as protease max that could theoretically improve protein extraction further.

That's about everything you should know to get started with nPOP! Please checkout the paper if you have not already, and move on to the protocol.io if you'd like to try it out.
